## CHAPTER 1: INTRODUCTION

### 1.1 Background and Motivation
Clear-Air Turbulence (CAT) remains one of the principal unresolved hazards in commercial and military aviation. While Numerical Weather Prediction (NWP) models are the standard for aviation forecasting, their disproportionately large grid sizes cannot resolve sub-grid turbulence eddies. Furthermore, the challenges of long-term temporal prediction have yet to be rigorously studied, leaving a critical gap in extended flight safety planning."

We propose a Dynamic Spatio-Temporal Graph Convolutional Network (ST-GCN) that transforms cooperative UAV swarms into a high-resolution atmospheric sensing grid. By exploiting dense, in-situ measurements, the model predicts the Eddy Dissipation Rate (EDR) at voxel resolution across the swarm’s operational volume. We move beyond static architectures by implementing dynamic soft adjacency, where edge weights are updated in real-time to reflect the changing spatial relationships of the UAV swarm. This allows the ST-GCN to capture fluid atmospheric dynamics across an evolving sensing grid, maintaining predictive continuity despite rapid node displacement.

To ensure these predictions are actionable for flight safety, the framework moves beyond point estimates by integrating Predictive Uncertainty Quantification. We implement a specialized output head that simultaneously predicts mean EDR and per-voxel log-variance, optimized via Gaussian Negative Log-Likelihood (NLL). This approach enables the model to self-calibrate its confidence levels without requiring ground-truth variance labels, effectively flagging regions where sparse data or extreme turbulence render predictions unreliable.

The high-fidelity targets for this uncertainty-aware model are generated directly on the Edge Hardware of each UAV. By fusing three independent estimation techniques—structure function, power spectral density, and variance methods—each node computes a robust median EDR from its own wind time-series. This decentralized pre-processing allows the swarm to generate its own "self-labeled" training data in real-time, eliminating the need for expensive external meteorological sensors.

However, the sensitive nature of atmospheric data and the institutional separation of operators make centralized data pooling impossible. We bridge this gap using a Federated Learning (FL) protocol powered by the FedProx algorithm. By transmitting only model weight deltas rather than raw data, we maintain strict privacy and security. Furthermore, by utilizing proximal regularization, our protocol successfully mitigates "client drift," ensuring the global model remains stable and accurate even when deployed across meteorologically heterogeneous regions.

### 1.2 Research Objectives
1. Physics-Informed Feature Engineering and Temporal Compression: We developed a 24-feature input pipeline that fuses raw sensor readings with physics-derived turbulence indices, such as the Ellrod Index and Richardson Number. To make 72-hour long-range forecasting feasible on edge devices, we designed a multi-scale temporal buffer. This strategy applies a moving average compression to historical features, significantly reducing the storage footprint while preserving the synoptic-scale trends necessary for extended predictions.
2. Dynamic Graph Representation via Soft Adjacency: To represent the moving UAV swarm, we implemented a Gaussian kernel soft adjacency formulation. Unlike static graphs or hard-distance thresholds that create step-function discontinuities, our approach provides smooth, differentiable edge weights as a function of inter-UAV distance. These weights are recomputed at every forward pass with $O(N^2)$ complexity. This ensures that the model’s "digital twin" of the atmosphere remains continuous and stable even as platforms maneuver rapidly across the operational volume.
3. Spatio-Temporal Model Architecture: We constructed a Spatio-Temporal Graph Convolutional Network (ST-GCN) optimized for multi-step forecasting. By stacking temporal encodings with our dynamic spatial graph, the model learns to identify complex atmospheric patterns across the 72-hour look-back window. This architecture allows the swarm to act as a high-resolution sensing grid, predicting the Eddy Dissipation Rate (EDR) at voxel resolution by capturing both localized eddies and broader meteorological shifts.
4. Uncertainty-Aware Training and Loss Function: To ensure predictions are actionable for flight safety, we implemented a specialized output head that provides calibrated uncertainty quantification. We optimized the network using a Gaussian Negative Log-Likelihood (NLL) loss, which enables the model to predict both a mean EDR and a per-voxel log-variance. This formulation allows for self-calibrated confidence estimates without requiring labeled variance ground truth. Training targets are supplied by an onboard EDR estimation module that fuses three independent methods—structure function, power spectral density, and variance—to provide robust, self-labeled ground truth in real-time.
5. Edge Hardware Implementation and Benchmarking: We validated the computational feasibility of this entire pipeline on commodity edge hardware, specifically the Raspberry Pi 5 and Jetson Nano. Through a device-simulation benchmark, we quantified inference latency and memory consumption, ensuring that the 72-hour feature cache and ST-GCN forward pass remain well within the 10-second operational cycle budget. Our results demonstrate that the framework can perform complex spatiotemporal reasoning without the need for centralized GPU acceleration.
6. Federated Learning Protocol via FedProx: Finally, we established a decentralized training protocol to enable multi-operator collaboration without compromising data privacy. We utilized the FedProx algorithm to manage model updates across geographically and institutionally separated swarms. By tuning the proximal regularization coefficient ($\mu_{prox}$), we successfully mitigated "client drift," ensuring the global model remains stable even when trained on meteorologically heterogeneous data from different regional deployments.

### 1.3 Thesis Organisation
The remainder of this paper is organised as follows. Chapter 2 reviews the related literature across five domains: classical turbulence diagnostics and NWP-based forecasting systems, machine learning for turbulence prediction, graph neural networks for spatio-temporal atmospheric modelling, UAV atmospheric sensing, and federated learning for distributed physical systems.
