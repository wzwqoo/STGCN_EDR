## CHAPTER 5: FUTURE WORK

### 5.1 Phase 1 Direction 1: Temporal Horizon and Feature Decay

#### 5.1.1 Current Limitation
Most current research in decentralized atmospheric sensing casually selects a fixed, arbitrary forecasting horizon—typically 1 to 6 hours—without a rigorous mathematical basis for the choice. However, the chaotic nature of turbulence means that the predictive power of a model is not uniform across time. Furthermore, the 24 features we use (Group A and B) likely possess differing "decay rates." For example, high-frequency wind shear (Group A) may offer high precision for short-term 5-minute "nowcasts," while synoptic-scale thermodynamic indices like CAPE or the K-Index (Group B) are likely more influential for the 24- to 72-hour windows. Treating all features as equally relevant across all timesteps leads to "feature noise," where short-term fluctuations degrade the stability of long-term forecasts.

#### 5.1.2 Research Direction
Future research should move beyond static horizons to study the optimal prediction window for each category of atmospheric phenomena. We propose a systematic evaluation of model performance across a continuous range of timesteps ($t+1h$ to $t+72h$) to identify the "prediction cliff" where GNN accuracy drops below baseline persistence. Additionally, a feature-importance decay study should be conducted using SHAP or GNNExplainer. By quantifying which indices retain predictive power at different temporal scales, we can develop a Multi-Scale Gated Architecture that dynamically weights features based on the target forecast horizon, ensuring the ST-GCN ignores high-frequency jitter when making long-range 72-hour projections.

### 5.2 Phase 1 Direction 2: Extended-Range Forecasting via Stratospheric Data Assimilation

#### 5.2.1 Current Limitation
Our current ST-GCN framework is vertically constrained to the troposphere, where UAV swarms typically operate. However, long-horizon turbulence forecasting is heavily influenced by stratosphere-troposphere coupling, particularly the downward propagation of planetary waves. A critical omission in current decentralized models is the impact of Sudden Stratospheric Warming (SSW) events and the Quasi-Biennial Oscillation (QBO). During an SSW, the polar vortex breaks down or displaces, causing a rapid increase in stratospheric temperatures and a subsequent reversal of zonal winds. Similarly, the phase of the QBO—the periodic reversal of the equatorial stratospheric easterlies and westerlies—modulates the "waveguide" for planetary waves, shifting the probability of turbulence outbreaks globally. By ignoring these high-altitude precursors and Potential Vorticity (PV) anomalies, the model lacks the "top-down" boundary conditions necessary to anticipate the high-energy turbulence regimes that follow stratospheric disruptions.

#### 5.2.2 Research Direction
A critical expansion of this work involves the integration of Stratospheric Data Assimilation into the ST-GCN’s temporal encoding layers, specifically targeting SSW and QBO-related anomalies. By treating the stratosphere as a low-frequency "master node," the model can identify the initial signal of a polar vortex weakening or a QBO phase shift up to 72 hours before it manifests as localized tropospheric turbulence. This research will evaluate the vertical information transfer between sparse, high-altitude satellite observations and dense, low-altitude swarm measurements to determine if stratospheric "precursors" can significantly extend the reliable forecast horizon beyond current limits.


### 5.3 Phase 1 Direction 3: Adaptive Swarm Deployment and Active Sensing

#### 5.3.1 Current Limitation
The current framework assumes a passively deployed swarm: UAVs are positioned at fixed grid locations and the model predicts turbulence given the current configuration. The feedback loop from prediction to deployment — specifically, using the model's uncertainty output to direct UAVs toward regions of high predictive uncertainty and thereby improve future predictions — is absent. The swarm is a passive observer rather than an active information-gathering agent.

#### 5.3.2 Research Direction
Active sensing, also termed informative path planning or Bayesian optimisation over sensor placement, represents one of the most scientifically impactful extensions of the proposed system. The log-variance output of the ST-GCN provides a direct measure of where additional observations would most reduce prediction uncertainty: regions with high σ are informative targets. A reinforcement learning agent trained to maximise the total information gain over the swarm's operational volume — measured by the reduction in total log-variance integrated over all voxels — could autonomously direct UAV redeployment in response to evolving atmospheric conditions.

The multi-agent nature of the problem — each UAV must plan its path considering the planned paths of all other UAVs to avoid redundant sampling — motivates a multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) formulation. The graph structure of the ST-GCN provides a natural communication channel for MARL: each UAV agent observes not only its own state and the current uncertainty map but also the planned trajectories of its graph neighbours, enabling decentralised execution with coordination through message passing. The energy budget constraint — each UAV must return to base before its battery is depleted — introduces a finite horizon planning requirement that will be addressed through hierarchical RL with a high-level mission planner and a low-level flight controller.

For weather balloon nodes, active sensing has a different character: the balloon's ascent rate and burst altitude can be adjusted through ballast release and valve control to dwell longer in regions of high atmospheric variability. The integration of adaptive balloon flight profiles with UAV active sensing creates a heterogeneous multi-scale active observation system that simultaneously covers the boundary layer, lower troposphere, and stratosphere.

### 5.4 Phase 2: Integrated Sensing, Computation, and Communication for Self-Organised Aerial Networks

#### 5.4.1 Limitations of Phase 1 Communication Architecture
The federated learning framework in Chapter 3 assumes a star topology: each client UAV swarm communicates directly with a central aggregation server, and individual UAVs within a swarm communicate via a local mesh network. This architecture has two fundamental limitations at ELA scale. First, the bandwidth required to transmit model weight deltas from all participating operators to a central server scales linearly with the number of model parameters and the number of clients, consuming a significant fraction of the available uplink spectrum as the network scales to hundreds of operators. Second, the central aggregation server represents a single point of failure: its unavailability — due to network outage, adversarial interference, or geographic coverage gaps over oceans — prevents model updates from propagating to airborne clients.

#### 5.4.2 Self-Organised Decentralised Federated Learning
Phase 2 will investigate fully decentralised federated learning architectures in which weight aggregation is performed peer-to-peer between neighbouring aerial nodes rather than through a central server. Gossip-based federated learning (Hegedus et al., 2021) propagates model updates through the aerial network by having each node average its weights with a randomly selected neighbour at each communication round. Convergence is guaranteed under mild connectivity assumptions and does not require any node to maintain the full global model state.

The dynamic graph structure of the ST-GCN provides a natural substrate for gossip protocol implementation: the same soft adjacency edge weights that govern atmospheric information exchange during inference can be repurposed to prioritise model update exchange during training rounds. UAV pairs with high edge weight — spatially close, observing similar atmospheric conditions — exchange weight updates more frequently, creating a spatially localised model that captures regional atmospheric characteristics while the global model improves through the gradual diffusion of updates across the network topology.

#### 5.4.3 Integrated Sensing and Communication (ISAC)
Beyond just sending data, the radio signals used for UAV-to-UAV and UAV-to-ground links can act like a radar. As these signals pass through turbulent air, they undergo subtle changes—shifting in frequency (Doppler shift), bouncing off atmospheric layers (delay spread), and arriving at different angles.By analyzing these fluctuations, we can calculate the refractive index structure constant ($C_n^2$), a value that tells us how much the air is "shimmering." Using microwave scintillation theory, we can translate these radio distortions into an independent measurement of turbulence (EDR) without adding any new hardware to the drone.

In Phase 2, we will develop algorithms that allow the UAV's antennas to do two things at once. Instead of choosing between a strong data connection and accurate weather sensing, we will use joint beamforming. For drones with multiple antennas, we can "split" the signal’s focus: some antennas handle high-speed data transfer, while others are dedicated to scanning the atmosphere. The system will automatically prioritize which function needs more power in real-time.

The data from this radio-sensing system will be fed directly into the ST-GCN (the neural network). This "virtual" sensor effectively extends the reach of the UAV. While physical sensors can only measure the air touching the drone, the ISAC system can "see" turbulence in the space between drones, significantly expanding the sensing range and accuracy of the entire swarm.

#### 5.4.4 Context-Aware Self-Organisation
A self-organised aerial network must adapt its communication topology, sensing strategy, computation allocation, and flight formation in response to changing atmospheric and mission contexts without external coordination. Phase 2 will investigate context-aware self-organisation protocols in which each aerial node infers its context — current atmospheric state, battery level, link quality, neighbour positions, and mission priority — from its local sensor observations and adapts its behaviour accordingly.

The uncertainty output of the ST-GCN provides a principled context signal: a node with high predicted EDR uncertainty in its voxel patch should increase its sampling rate, attract neighbouring UAVs toward its location, and increase the frequency of model update exchanges. Conversely, a node in calm, well-observed air can reduce its sampling rate, conserve energy, and defer to its neighbours' model updates. This uncertainty-driven self-organisation creates an emergent collective behaviour in which the aerial network automatically concentrates its resources — sensors, computation, and communication bandwidth — in regions of high atmospheric activity.
